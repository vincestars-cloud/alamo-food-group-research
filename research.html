Pain Points — Exact Voice of Customer

#1: "I'm Just a Number to Them" UNIVERSAL

"I understand that they have a lot of other deliveries and that I tend to be small fish compared to most of their accounts and they don't give a shit, that my place isn't a priority. That's the f---ing problem!! They don't give a shit since once they've got you, they've got you."— Restaurant owner, CulinaryAnarchy.com
"Smaller restaurants have almost no negotiating power with Sysco, and if you are in a rural area where there aren't any other distributors, then restaurants really don't have many options at all."— Delivisor aggregated reviews

#2: Delivery Nightmares EXTREMELY HIGH

"We almost never get an order that has everything that we ordered in it."— Restaurant owner, PissedConsumer (Sysco)
"They might have refrigerated trucks but that doesn't do anything when they leave your products sitting outside your back door."— CulinaryAnarchy.com
"From mislabeled products to delayed, half-fulfilled and canceled deliveries, valley restaurants are selling out of products and wasting money paying employees to work when the food they're paid to prepare hasn't arrived."— Jackson Hole news report

#3: Hidden Fees & Price Gouging VERY HIGH

"If the salmon I agreed to buy at $4.99 a pound a month ago is now showing up on my invoice at $6 a pound, I've just upped my plate cost by 75 cents."— RestaurantBusinessOnline

FoodServiceIQ exposes 15-30% distributor markups and opaque contracts. Sysco paper products priced "double or triple" vs competitors.

#4: Reps Who Don't Care HIGH

"They are not looking out for your best interests at all, they're looking at what it's going to take to get that new black Mercedes."— CulinaryAnarchy.com (about Sysco reps)

#5: Substitutions Without Warning HIGH

"Regularly ordered products like chicken thighs or specific cheese brands are often unavailable, disrupting menu planning and food preparation."— Delivisor aggregated reviews

A Vermont restaurant owner had to "drive to a Whole Foods 3.5 hours away in Boston and pay twice the wholesale cost" after Sysco halted an order last-minute.

#6: Contract Lock-in & Minimums HIGH for small operators

Sysco: $500 order minimum. Order minimums have risen to the point that most operators have narrowed their supplier base to 1-2 just to meet them.

Fear #3: Rising Food Costs They Can't Control

"It's literally everything. Beans are up, rice are up."— Ricardo Lopez, La Vaca Birria owner
81% of Mexican restaurant owners said food costs are up. Meat, chicken and cheese led increases. Paper products described as "crazy expensive." Tripas jumped from $0.69/lb to $2.29/lb.

What They Wish Existed

1. A Distributor Who Actually Knows Them

"The top reasons operators change [distributors] are communication and people. You have to have a comfort level with the people, and there have to be strong, open channels of communication."— US Foodservice VP

Personnel changes at the distributor often trigger operators to leave — the human relationship IS the retention mechanism.

2. Flexible, No Contracts, No Minimums

What owners love about Restaurant Depot: "A small restaurant owner can walk in and pay wholesale with no contract, no sales rep, and no delivery minimum."

3. Transparent, Honest Pricing

Owners want to understand all fees, incentives, rebates, and administrative charges. The fact that advice like "every invoice should be checked against your contracts" exists shows how opaque the industry is.

4. Authentic Hispanic/Mexican Products Under One Roof

One-stop shop for specialty chili peppers, cheeses, quality proteins, fresh produce, tortillas, spices. Distributors who understand authentic Mexican cuisine vs. generic "Tex-Mex" substitutes.

40% of Hispanics say bilingual communications are important to them.

5. Reliability Above All

"Not getting what you want, when or how you want it and/or at the agreed-upon price" is the #1 trigger for switching distributors. — RestaurantBusinessOnline

Why Restaurants Don't Switch Suppliers

FrictionWhat It Actually Means
Credit relationshipSwitching = starting at COD or prepay. For thin-cash restaurants, this alone kills the conversation.
Rebates & volume incentivesQuarterly rebates on volume tiers. Switching mid-quarter loses rebate progress.
SKU specification matchingChef's recipes built around specific brands — not "queso fresco" but a specific brand with specific moisture content.
Existing inventory commitmentsConsigned inventory, weekly standing orders, short-dated promo deals.
Relationship capitalThe current rep has been to the owner's daughter's quinceañera. The dispatcher answers texts at 9pm.
Operational riskFirst 60 days = highest risk of shorts and substitutions. A miss on Friday night is catastrophic.
Switching laborSet up new vendor in POS, accounting, receiving SOP. Owner-operators don't have the hours.
The counter-offer dynamic: Diaz, Gordo's, Multimex all have account managers compensated on retention. The moment a restaurant signals weakening, the incumbent counters with: price cut on top SKUs, deferred AR payments, personal visit from senior person, bundled deal locking in 12 more months.

Industry Churn Data

  • 20-30% annual churn in foodservice distribution sales volume (Zilliant)
  • Churn is often "silent" — customers gradually shift orders without formally canceling
  • Independents evaluate suppliers every 12-24 months
  • Many maintain 2-3 supplier relationships simultaneously to hedge risk

Diaz Foods

Claim: "Serving the needs of the Hispanic culinary market since 1969"

Positioning: Legacy, scale, nationwide reach, BRC certification

Gap: No emotional/personal messaging. Purely institutional. Vulnerable on "we're too big to care."

Souto Foods

Claim: "Complete Hispanic retail solution"

Positioning: Consultative — category management, market insights, training

Gap: Retail-focused, not pure foodservice. New — no deep trust yet.

Sysco

Claim: "At the heart of food and service"

Positioning: Scale, reliability, technology, national reach

Gap: They're the villain in every restaurant owner's story. Everything in Tab 3.

Restaurant Depot

Claim: "Where restaurants shop"

Positioning: No-contract, wholesale pricing, self-service

Gap: No delivery, long lines, rude staff, empty shelves, being acquired by Sysco.
